Foundation

Understanding Double Materiality

The conceptual, regulatory, and strategic foundation of a rigorous materiality assessment.

Double Materiality is the principle that a company must assess sustainability from two simultaneous directions: the impact its operations have on people and the environment, and the impact sustainability issues have on its own financial performance. A topic is material if it is significant in either or both dimensions.

🌍
Inside-Out

Impact Materiality. How does your organisation affect people, communities, and the environment? This includes actual and potential impacts, both negative and positive, across your entire value chain from upstream sourcing to downstream use.

Governed by ESRS 1 and GRI 3. Assessed through Scale, Scope, Irremediability, and Likelihood.

Outside-In

Financial Materiality. How do sustainability conditions affect your organisation's financial position, revenues, costs, assets, and access to capital? This includes risks and opportunities arising from environmental, social, and governance factors.

Aligned with IFRS Sustainability and ESRS 1. Assessed through Magnitude and Likelihood of financial effects.

ESRS 1 European Sustainability Reporting Standards Mandatory under the EU's CSRD. Requires both impact and financial materiality assessment. Defines the Double Materiality concept in regulatory terms.
GRI 3 Global Reporting Initiative: Material Topics Focuses on impact materiality through stakeholder engagement and due diligence. A GRI assessment forms a strong foundation for the impact dimension of DMA.
BRSR Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting SEBI's mandatory framework for India's top 1,000 listed companies. Requires disclosure on nine NGRBC principles with quantitative ESG KPIs.
EFRAG IG 1 Materiality Assessment Implementation Guidance Provides step-by-step guidance on how to conduct a DMA under ESRS. Defines thresholds, IRO identification, and stakeholder engagement requirements.
🇮🇳

Mandatory from FY 2022-23: SEBI requires India's top 1,000 listed companies to file a Business Responsibility and Sustainability Report (BRSR) covering environmental, social, and governance performance across nine NGRBC principles. From FY 2025-26, value chain disclosures and limited assurance apply to the top 250 listed entities.

Regulatory

SEBI and the BRSR Mandate

Introduced in May 2021 to replace the Business Responsibility Report, the BRSR is India's most comprehensive ESG disclosure framework. It requires quantitative KPIs, board oversight disclosures, and reporting on stakeholder grievance mechanisms across all nine principles of responsible business conduct. Unlike earlier BRR requirements, BRSR demands granular, verifiable data that can be independently assured.

Global Alignment

ESRS and GRI: Why They Matter for Indian Companies

Indian companies with European operations or supply chain relationships are directly affected by the EU's CSRD and the associated ESRS standards. Even companies without direct EU exposure increasingly face investor and customer expectations aligned with ESRS and GRI. A Double Materiality Assessment conducted under this framework satisfies both BRSR and global reporting requirements simultaneously.

Strategic Value

From Compliance to Competitive Advantage

A significant proportion of Indian sustainability professionals treat DMA as a compliance exercise rather than a strategic tool. Organisations that conduct a genuine, stakeholder-validated DMA gain a structured understanding of where sustainability creates financial risk, where it creates opportunity, and where their operations cause the greatest harm. That understanding drives better capital allocation and stronger ESG ratings.

Assurance Ready

Building an Audit Trail from Day One

SEBI's BRSR Core framework (2023) introduced limited assurance requirements for key ESG KPIs. A structured DMA with documented stakeholder engagement, weighted relevance scores, and an IRO Register creates the evidence trail that assurance providers require. Organisations without this structure face significant rework when assurance becomes mandatory.

Most DMA processes in Indian practice are peer-benchmarked rather than evidence-based, resulting in near-identical materiality lists across organisations regardless of what they actually do or who bears the consequences of their activities. This framework was designed to make that outcome structurally improbable.

Standard Practice

How Most DMAs Are Done

  • Topics selected by benchmarking peers, not by evidence
  • All stakeholders surveyed on all topics regardless of relevance
  • Responses averaged without weighting for influence or expertise
  • Impact and financial dimensions assessed simultaneously, compromising independence
  • No structured IRO Register: scoring based on implicit assumptions
  • No governance checkpoints: one team, one pass, no independent validation
This Framework

Stakeholder-Centric DMA

  • Enterprise context sets the scope before any topic is evaluated
  • Binary Relevance Filter: only stakeholders with documented connection to a topic contribute scores
  • Stakeholder Weight calibrates influence by Power and Interest, not by response volume
  • IRO Register formally documents each impact, risk, and opportunity before scoring begins
  • Impact and financial dimensions assessed independently and approved separately
  • ESG SteerCo governance at six defined checkpoints throughout the process

01
Foundation

Enterprise Context Setting and Material Topic Identification

Before any topic is evaluated, the organisation's specific operating reality must be formally established.

⬡ ESG SteerCo Checkpoint 1
What Happens

A formal analysis of the enterprise business context is conducted: the organisation's specific products and operations, its full value chain from upstream sourcing to downstream use, and its stated sustainability commitments. This context-first sequencing is mandated by the Natural Resource Based View, which requires environmental strategies to be anchored in an organisation's specific resource constraints and operating environment.

Why It Matters

Most organisations produce near-identical materiality topic lists by benchmarking peers rather than examining their own operations. This step makes that outcome structurally improbable. No topic enters the assessment without a documented link to the organisation's specific value chain activities. The result is a material topic list that is genuinely organisational, not inherited from convention. Aligned with GRI Standard 3 and ESRS 1 AR16.

Output
Deliverable

A validated longlist of material topics mapped to the organisation's value chain, with documented sustainability commitments and regulatory context. Approved by ESG SteerCo before advancing to Step 2.

02
Stakeholders

Stakeholder Identification and Prioritisation

Distinguishing who bears the consequences from who reads the reports.

⬡ ESG SteerCo Checkpoints 2 and 3
What Happens

All stakeholders are identified and separated into two structural categories: Affected Stakeholders, those whose interests are impacted by the organisation's activities, and Users of Reports, those who use sustainability information for decision-making. Each stakeholder is then scored on Power and Interest on a scale of 1 to 10, producing a Stakeholder Weight using Equations 4.1 and 4.2. The Power vs Interest Matrix visualises the prioritisation.

Why It Matters

Stakeholder lists are typically shaped by who is most visible to management, usually investors and customers. Affected communities and marginalised groups are routinely absent. This structural separation corrects commercial stakeholder dominance directly. In Indian practice, NGOs, civil society, and labour unions influence fewer than 5% of materiality processes. The quantitative weight ensures no single group dominates by response volume alone.

Output
Deliverable

A prioritised stakeholder register with Power scores, Interest scores, and calculated Stakeholder Weights. Divided into Key Stakeholders, Subject Matter Experts, and General Stakeholders. Independently validated by ESG SteerCo at two checkpoints.

03
Evidence Base

Materiality Topics and IRO Register

Converting dispersed organisational knowledge into a structured, auditable evidence framework.

⬡ ESG SteerCo Checkpoint 4
What Happens

Every material topic from Step 1 is mapped to its associated Impacts, Risks, and Opportunities (IROs). Each IRO is assigned defined boundaries, measurable KPI structures, and a documented position within the value chain. Key Stakeholders and Subject Matter Experts provide definitional inputs through two parallel channels: to the Material Topics list and to the IRO Register itself, with a feedback loop ensuring internal consistency.

Why It Matters

Material topics typically enter scoring without formally documented impacts or risks. Where risk identification exists, it remains narrative, vague, and disconnected from value chain activities. The IRO Register makes the assessment resistant to that failure by converting implicit assumptions into a structured, verifiable data framework. It is the mechanism through which every subsequent scoring decision becomes externally defensible. Data gaps are consistently cited as one of the most critical DMA implementation constraints in Indian practice.

Output
Deliverable

A complete IRO Register with every material topic assigned documented impact, risk, and opportunity boundaries alongside KPI structures. ESG SteerCo approval required before the register enters the scoring process in Steps 4 and 5.

04
Engagement

Stakeholder Relevance Mapping and Engagement

Replacing broad undifferentiated outreach with governed, evidence-generating engagement.

⬡ ESG SteerCo Checkpoint 5
What Happens

A Binary Relevance Filter is applied before any engagement begins. For each stakeholder-topic combination, a formal Yes/No decision is documented: only stakeholders with a substantive and documented connection to a topic contribute scores for that topic. Cleared stakeholders are then engaged through targeted surveys, interviews, focus groups, or workshops, and asked to rate each relevant topic on a scale of 1 to 10 for both Financial and Impact Materiality.

Why It Matters

Most surveys are distributed to every stakeholder on every topic, producing data dominated by whoever responds fastest rather than whoever is most relevant. The Binary Relevance Filter inverts that logic entirely. Stakeholder inputs are then weighted by the governance-validated weights from Step 2, using Equations 4.3 and 4.4 to produce Topic Relevance Scores. These scores form the Y-axis of both the Impact and Financial Materiality Maps.

Output
Deliverable

A Topic Relevance Score (TRS) for both Impact Materiality and Financial Materiality for each of the 21 material topics. Calculated using weighted aggregation across relevant stakeholders only. These scores feed directly into Step 5 as the Y-axis coordinates of all three materiality maps.

05
Assessment

Materiality Assessment

Three independent assessments: Impact, Financial, and Double Materiality.

⬡ ESG SteerCo Checkpoints 5 and 6
What Happens

Step 5A (Impact): Each topic is scored on Scale, Scope, Irremediability, and Likelihood by expert assessors. Four equations apply depending on whether the impact is Actual Negative, Potential Negative, Actual Positive, or Potential Positive (Equations 4.5a to 4.5d). Step 5B (Financial): Each topic's Risk or Opportunity is scored on Magnitude and Likelihood (Equations 4.6a and 4.6b). Step 5C (Double): Both dimensions are combined into a single Double Materiality Map using Equations 4.7 and 4.8.

Why It Matters

Impact and financial dimensions are assessed independently and approved separately by ESG SteerCo before being combined. This prevents the more familiar financial dimension from dominating the process at the expense of the less familiar impact dimension. In practice, the financial dimension tends to receive significantly greater attention than impact materiality in Indian organisations, and a common misconception is that DMA is concerned only with environmental and social impacts, leaving the financial dimension absent or subordinated.

Output
Deliverable

Three validated materiality maps: Impact Materiality Map, Financial Materiality Map, and Double Materiality Map. Each topic is positioned by two coordinates derived from expert assessment and stakeholder relevance scores combined. Topics above the materiality threshold proceed to full disclosure under ESRS, GRI, or BRSR as applicable.

Methodology and Sources

The scoring logic, regulatory grounding, and data transparency behind every number in this dashboard.

Standards and Frameworks

ESRS 1
European Sustainability Reporting Standards — General Requirements Defines Double Materiality as the obligation to assess both impact materiality (Inside-Out) and financial materiality (Outside-In). Requires application of appropriate quantitative or qualitative thresholds. Mandates ESG SteerCo-equivalent governance oversight of the materiality determination process.
GRI 3
GRI Standard 3: Material Topics 2021 Governs the impact materiality dimension. Requires organisations to identify actual and potential negative and positive impacts across their full value chain through genuine stakeholder engagement. Severity of negative impacts is determined by Scale, Scope, and Irremediable character. Likelihood is additionally assessed for potential impacts.
EFRAG IG1
Materiality Assessment Implementation Guidance — May 2024 Provides authoritative step-by-step guidance for conducting a DMA under ESRS. Financial materiality thresholds are based on a combination of likelihood of occurrence and magnitude of financial effects across short, medium, and long-term horizons. A topic may be financially material even when effects are not yet reliably measurable, in which case qualitative thresholds apply.
BRSR
SEBI Business Responsibility and Sustainability Reporting — 2021 India's mandatory ESG disclosure framework for the top 1,000 listed companies. Requires quantitative KPI reporting across nine NGRBC principles. BRSR Core (2023) introduced limited assurance requirements for key ESG indicators applicable to the top 250 listed entities from FY 2025-26.
THESIS
Telang, S.A. (2026). Double Materiality Assessment: A Stakeholder-Centric Process for the Indian Industrial Context. Master's Thesis, Karlsruhe Institute of Technology (KIT), Institute of Production Science (wbk). The five-step framework, stakeholder weighting methodology, Binary Relevance Filter, and IRO Register structure underpinning this dashboard are sourced from Chapter 4 and Appendix E of this work.

Thresholds and What They Mean

The Scoring Scale — 1 to 10
Low · 1 to 3 Marginal impact or financial effect. Limited in scale, unlikely to materialise, or easily remediated. Topics in this band are monitored but do not require immediate disclosure or action.
Medium · 4 to 7 Moderate significance. The effect is present and acknowledged but not severe enough to be decisive on its own. Many topics cluster here. Context and stakeholder weighting determine whether they cross the materiality line.
High · 8 to 10 Major or life-threatening impacts on people or the environment, or large-scale financial effects on revenues, costs, assets, or access to capital. Topics scoring here are material by any reasonable threshold.
Impact Materiality Threshold
What makes an impact material Per ESRS 1 Chapter 3.4 and GRI 3, a negative impact is material when its severity is significant. Severity is determined by how grave the impact is (Scale), how many people or how much of the environment is affected (Scope), and whether the harm can be reversed or remediated (Irremediability). For potential impacts, the probability of occurrence (Likelihood) is also assessed.
Positive impacts Positive impacts are assessed on Scale and Scope only. Irremediability does not apply to benefits. This follows EFRAG IG1 guidance, which structurally excludes irremediability from positive impact scoring.
Threshold applied here A combined Impact Materiality Score of 6.5 or above on a 1 to 10 scale. This threshold is organisation-defined and validated by the ESG SteerCo at Step 5C, consistent with ESRS 1 paragraph AR 15 which requires thresholds to be set and disclosed.
Financial Materiality Threshold
What makes a risk or opportunity material Per ESRS 1 paragraph AR 15, financial materiality is determined by the combination of Magnitude (how large the potential financial effect would be on revenues, costs, assets, liabilities, or access to capital) and Likelihood (how probable that effect is). Both risks and opportunities are assessed using the same two dimensions. A high-magnitude but low-likelihood risk carries equivalent analytical weight to a high-likelihood moderate-magnitude risk.
Time horizon Financial materiality thresholds must cover short, medium, and long-term horizons. A topic may be financially material even if its effects are not yet measurable at the reporting date. In such cases qualitative thresholds apply, per EFRAG IG1 paragraph 134.
Threshold applied here A combined Financial Materiality Score of 6.5 or above on a 1 to 10 scale, validated by the ESG SteerCo as a separate governance event from the Impact Materiality approval, ensuring the two dimensions are determined independently before being combined on the Double Materiality Map.
Double Materiality: Reading the Map
Upper right · Doubly material Topic scores above 6.5 on both axes. Significant from both the Inside-Out and Outside-In perspective. Full disclosure required under ESRS, GRI, or BRSR as applicable.
Upper left or lower right · Singly material Topic is material in one dimension only. Per ESRS 1 paragraph 33, singly material topics still require policy, action, and target disclosures even if full metric reporting is not triggered in the non-material dimension.
Lower left · Below threshold Topic scores below 6.5 on both axes. Not material in this reporting cycle. Flagged for reassessment in the next annual cycle as conditions and stakeholder views evolve.
